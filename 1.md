# Q1) The Probability of Shooting at a Target

## Problem Statement

A point is chosen at random from a disk of radius 10. Let **A** be the event that the point lies within 1 unit of the boundary.

1. Model the experiment assuming the point is **uniformly distributed with respect to area**, and compute P(A).
2. Model the experiment assuming the **distance to the point from the center is chosen uniformly** from [0, 10], and the direction is chosen independently and uniformly from [0, 2π), and compute P(A).
3. Explain why the two answers differ.

---

## Setup

Let the disk be:

```math
\Omega = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x^2 + y^2 \leq 100\}
```

Let **A** be the event that the chosen point lies within 1 unit of the boundary. Since the boundary is the circle of radius 10, the point must have distance **at least 9** and **at most 10** from the center. Thus:

```math
A = \{(x, y) \in \Omega : 9 \leq \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \leq 10\}
```

So **A** is the **outer annulus** of the disk.

---

## Part 1 — Uniform Distribution with Respect to Area

In this model, equal areas are assigned equal probabilities. The probability of any event is its area divided by the total area of the disk.

**Total area of the disk:**

```math
\text{Area}(\Omega) = \pi (10)^2 = 100\pi
```

**Area of event A** (annulus between radii 9 and 10):

```math
\text{Area}(A) = \pi(10)^2 - \pi(9)^2 = 100\pi - 81\pi = 19\pi
```

**Probability:**

```math
P(A) = \frac{\text{Area}(A)}{\text{Area}(\Omega)} = \frac{19\pi}{100\pi} = \frac{19}{100}
```

> **Answer: P(A) = 19/100**

---

## Part 2 — Uniform Distribution of the Radius

In this model, there are two independent variables:

- **Radius** r: chosen uniformly from [0, 10]
- **Angle** θ: chosen uniformly from [0, 2π)

Since the angle is independent of the radius, the direction does not affect whether the point falls near the boundary. We focus entirely on r.

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---|
| Total length of radius interval [0, 10] | 10 |
| Favorable interval for A: r in [9, 10] | 1 |

**Probability:**

```math
P(A) = \frac{\text{Favorable Length}}{\text{Total Length}} = \frac{10 - 9}{10 - 0} = \frac{1}{10}
```

> **Answer: P(A) = 1/10**

---

## Part 3 — Why the Two Answers Differ

The two answers differ because **the phrase "a point is chosen at random from a disk" does not determine a unique probability model**. The event A is the same geometric set in both cases, but the two models assign probability differently.

### Model 1 — Area-Uniform

Probability is proportional to **area**. The outer annulus [9, 10] is geometrically large, so it receives a high probability:

```math
P(A) = \frac{19}{100} = 19\%
```

Points near the boundary are *more likely* because they occupy more area.

### Model 2 — Radius-Uniform

Probability is proportional to **radial length** only. The interval [9, 10] is just 1 out of 10 equal units:

```math
P(A) = \frac{1}{10} = 10\%
```

This model ignores the fact that outer radii sweep more area.

### Root Cause

The key difference is the **area element** in polar coordinates:

```math
dA = r \, dr \, d\theta
```

The factor of **r** means that larger radii contribute *more area per unit of radius*. The area-uniform model accounts for this; the radius-uniform model ignores it.

> **In short:** the two models are different probability measures on the same sample space. The same event can have different probabilities under different measures. Probabilities are not determined by geometry alone — they also depend on *how* "random" is defined.

---

## Physical Interpretation

| Scenario | Appropriate Model | P(A) |
|---|---|---|
| Random dart throwing (no directional/radial bias) | Area-uniform | 19/100 |
| Two-step procedure: pick r uniformly, then pick θ | Radius-uniform | 1/10 |

In most physical experiments, **"randomly chosen point in a disk"** refers to the **area-uniform** model — because randomly scattered points naturally land in proportion to area. The radius-uniform model represents an artificial two-step sampling procedure, not ordinary geometric randomness.

# Q2) Bertrand's Paradox


## Preliminary Geometric Fact

Let the circle be:

```math
x^2 + y^2 = 1
```

The side length of the inscribed equilateral triangle is $\sqrt{3}$.

A chord is longer than $\sqrt{3}$ **if and only if** its perpendicular distance from the center is less than 1/2.

**Proof:**

If a chord is at distance $r \in [0, 1]$ from the center, then half its length is the horizontal leg of a right triangle with hypotenuse 1 and other leg $r$. Hence the chord length is:

```math
L(r) = 2\sqrt{1 - r^2}
```

We want $L(r) > \sqrt{3}$, which is equivalent to:

```math
r < \frac{1}{2}
```

So the event of interest is precisely:

```math
A = \{\text{distance of chord from center} < \tfrac{1}{2}\}
```

---

## Model 1 — Random Endpoint, Then Random Angle

**Procedure:** Choose one endpoint uniformly on the circumference, then choose the other endpoint uniformly on the circumference. By rotational symmetry, fix the first endpoint and let θ ∈ [0, π] be the subtended central angle.

**Probability Space:**

```math
\Omega_1 = [0, \pi], \quad P_1(E) = \frac{\lambda(E)}{\pi}
```

**Chord length in terms of θ:**

```math
L(\theta) = 2\sin(\theta/2)
```

We want $L(\theta) > \sqrt{3}$:

```math
2\sin(\theta/2) > \sqrt{3} \implies \sin(\theta/2) > \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \implies \theta > \frac{2\pi}{3}
```

So the favorable event is $A_1 = (2\pi/3,\ \pi]$.

**Probability:**

```math
P_1(A_1) = \frac{\pi - \frac{2\pi}{3}}{\pi} = \frac{1}{3}
```

> **Answer under Model 1: P = 1/3**

---

## Model 2 — Random Radius, Chord Perpendicular to It

**Procedure:** Choose a radius, then choose a point uniformly along that radius, and draw the chord perpendicular to the radius through that point. Equivalently, the distance $r$ of the chord from the center is chosen uniformly in [0, 1].

**Probability Space:**

```math
\Omega_2 = [0, 1], \quad P_2(E) = \lambda(E)
```

From the preliminary calculation, the chord exceeds $\sqrt{3}$ exactly when:

```math
r < \frac{1}{2}
```

So the favorable event is $A_2 = [0,\ 1/2)$.

**Probability:**

```math
P_2(A_2) = \lambda\!\left([0, \tfrac{1}{2})\right) = \frac{1}{2}
```

> **Answer under Model 2: P = 1/2**

---

## Model 3 — Random Midpoint Uniformly in the Disk

**Procedure:** Choose the midpoint of the chord uniformly from the whole disk, then draw the unique chord having that midpoint. Since every chord is uniquely determined by its midpoint, this defines a valid probability law on chords.

**Probability Space:**

```math
\Omega_3 = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x^2 + y^2 \leq 1\}, \quad P_3(E) = \frac{\lambda_2(E)}{\pi}
```

If the midpoint is $(x, y)$, its distance from the center is $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. The chord exceeds $\sqrt{3}$ exactly when $r < 1/2$, so:

```math
A_3 = \{(x, y) \in \Omega_3 : x^2 + y^2 < \tfrac{1}{4}\}
```

This is the disk of radius 1/2.

**Probability:**

```math
P_3(A_3) = \frac{\text{Area of disk of radius } \frac{1}{2}}{\text{Area of unit disk}} = \frac{\pi (1/2)^2}{\pi} = \frac{1}{4}
```

> **Answer under Model 3: P = 1/4**

---

## Summary

| Model | Parameter chosen uniformly | P(chord > √3) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Random endpoints | Subtended angle θ ∈ [0, π] | 1/3 |
| 2 — Random radius point | Distance from center r ∈ [0, 1] | 1/2 |
| 3 — Random midpoint | Midpoint in the unit disk | 1/4 |

---

## Conclusion

Each answer is **correct** for a different mathematically precise meaning of "choose a chord at random."

This is **Bertrand's Paradox**: the verbal description alone does not determine a unique probability model. One must specify the sampling procedure — equivalently, the probability space.

> The phrase "a chord is chosen at random" is ambiguous. Different natural probability spaces yield different answers: **1/3**, **1/2**, and **1/4**. This shows that probability is not determined by the geometry of the sample space alone, but also by the probability measure placed on it.



# Q8) Probability of Being a Girl?

## Sample Space

Let the first letter denote the **older child** and the second the **younger child**:

```math
\Omega = \{BB,\ BG,\ GB,\ GG\}
```

Each outcome has equal probability:

```math
P(\{BB\}) = P(\{BG\}) = P(\{GB\}) = P(\{GG\}) = \frac{1}{4}
```

---

## Solution

**Condition:** "At least one child is a girl" — this excludes only BB.

The reduced sample space becomes:

```math
\{BG,\ GB,\ GG\}
```

These three outcomes are equally likely. Among them, only **GG** satisfies "both are girls."

Applying the definition of conditional probability:

```math
P(\text{both girls} \mid \text{at least one girl}) = \frac{P(\{GG\})}{P(\{BG, GB, GG\})} = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \frac{1}{3}
```

> **Answer:**
> ```math
> P(\text{both girls} \mid \text{at least one girl}) = \frac{1}{3}
> ```

---

## Why It Is Interesting

Many people first guess **1/2**, but that corresponds to a **different question** — one where the girl is identified in a specific position.

### Comparison of Two Questions

**Question A:** At least one child is a girl (no position specified)

```math
\text{Reduced space: } \{BG, GB, GG\} \implies P = \frac{1}{3}
```

**Question B:** The **older** child is known to be a girl

```math
\text{Reduced space: } \{GB, GG\} \implies P(\text{both girls} \mid \text{older is a girl}) = \frac{1}{2}
```

---

## Summary Table

| Given Information | Reduced Sample Space | P(both girls) |
|---|---|---|
| At least one is a girl | {BG, GB, GG} | 1/3 |
| The older child is a girl | {GB, GG} | 1/2 |
| The younger child is a girl | {BG, GG} | 1/2 |

---

## Key Takeaway

> The answer depends **crucially on how the information is given**, not just on what the information tells us about the children.
>
> Saying "at least one is a girl" leaves three equally likely outcomes. Saying "the older child is a girl" pins down a position and leaves only two. This is a classic illustration of how **conditional probability is sensitive to the structure of the conditioning event**, not just its content.


# Q9) Information Available in a Discrete Random Variable

This document provides a comprehensive analysis of two random variables, $X$ and $Y$, defined on the same probability space. We explore their $\sigma$-algebras, joint distributions, and Shannon Entropy to demonstrate how $Y$ carries more information than $X$.

## 1. Probability Space Definition
The sample space $\Omega$ represents products categorized by quality ($G$=Good, $D$=Defective) and the machine that produced them (1 or 2).

- **Sample Space**: $\Omega = \{G_1, G_2, D_1, D_2\}$
- **Sigma-algebra**: $\mathfrak{F} = \mathfrak{P}(\Omega)$ (the power set of $\Omega$)
- **Probability Measure $P$**:
  - $P(\{G_1\}) = 0.50$
  - $P(\{G_2\}) = 0.20$
  - $P(\{D_1\}) = 0.10$
  - $P(\{D_2\}) = 0.20$

$\sum_{\omega \in \Omega} P(\omega) = 0.50 + 0.20 + 0.10 + 0.20 = 1.0$, confirming a valid measure.

## 2. Sigma-Algebras and Information Content

### Random Variable $X$ (Quality Only)
$X$ maps outcomes to $\{0, 1\}$ where 0 is good and 1 is defective.
- $X^{-1}(\{0\}) = \{G_1, G_2\}$
- $X^{-1}(\{1\}) = \{D_1, D_2\}$
- **$\sigma(X)$**: $\{\varnothing, \Omega, \{G_1, G_2\}, \{D_1, D_2\}\}$

### Random Variable $Y$ (Quality & Machine)
$Y$ maps every outcome to a unique integer $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$.
- **$\sigma(Y)$**: $\mathfrak{P}(\Omega)$ (Contains all $2^4 = 16$ possible subsets).

### Comparison
Since every set in $\sigma(X)$ is a member of $\mathfrak{P}(\Omega)$, we have:
$$\sigma(X) \subseteq \sigma(Y)$$
This proves that $Y$ is **finer** than $X$. $Y$ allows us to distinguish between machines, whereas $X$ only allows us to distinguish quality.

## 3. Distributions and Conditional Probabilities

### Marginals
- **$P(X=0)$** $= 0.50 + 0.20 = 0.70$
- **$P(X=1)$** $= 0.10 + 0.20 = 0.30$
- **$P(Y=1)=0.50, P(Y=2)=0.20, P(Y=3)=0.10, P(Y=4)=0.20$**

### Joint Probabilities
The variables are coupled; $Y$ completely determines $X$:
- $P(X=0, Y=1) = 0.50$
- $P(X=0, Y=2) = 0.20$
- $P(X=1, Y=3) = 0.10$
- $P(X=1, Y=4) = 0.20$

### Conditionals
1. **$P(X=1 | Y=3) = 1$**: Knowing the item is from Machine 1 and is defective ($Y=3$) leaves no doubt that it is defective ($X=1$).
2. **$P(Y=3 | X=1) = \frac{0.10}{0.30} = \frac{1}{3}$**: Knowing only that an item is defective does not identify the machine; there is still uncertainty.

## 4. Shannon Entropy Calculations
Using the natural logarithm (nats): $H(Z) = -\sum P(z) \ln P(z)$.

| Metric | Value (approx.) | Interpretation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $H(X)$ | $0.6109$ | Uncertainty regarding quality. |
| $H(Y)$ | $1.2206$ | Uncertainty regarding quality AND machine. |
| $H(X, Y)$ | $1.2206$ | Total uncertainty of the system (same as $Y$). |
| $H(X | Y)$ | $0.0000$ | $Y$ contains all info needed to know $X$. |
| $H(Y | X)$ | $0.6097$ | Remaining uncertainty about the machine after knowing quality. |

## 5. Python Visualization
The following code generates a heatmap of the joint distribution and a comparison of the partitions induced by the two variables.

```python
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

# Data setup
omega = ["G1", "G2", "D1", "D2"]
p = np.array([0.50, 0.20, 0.10, 0.20])
joint = np.array([[0.50, 0.20, 0.00, 0.00], [0.00, 0.00, 0.10, 0.20]])

# Plotting
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 4))
im = ax.imshow(joint, cmap="YlOrRd", aspect="auto")

# Labels
ax.set_xticks(range(4))
ax.set_xticklabels([1, 2, 3, 4])
ax.set_yticks(range(2))
ax.set_yticklabels([0, 1])
ax.set_xlabel("Y (Machine & Quality)")
ax.set_ylabel("X (Quality)")
ax.set_title("Joint Distribution P(X, Y)")

for i in range(2):
    for j in range(4):
        ax.text(j, i, f"{joint[i, j]:.2f}", ha="center", va="center")

plt.colorbar(im, label="Probability")
plt.show()

# 📄 `theory.md`
```md
# Theory Summary

## Probability Space
Ω = ℝ²  
F = B(ℝ²)  
P defined by:

f(t,p) = (1 / 4π) exp(-t²/2 - p²/8)

---

## Random Variables
X(t,p) = t  
Y(t,p) = (t,p)

---

## Sigma-Algebras

σ(X) = { B × ℝ : B ∈ B(ℝ) }  
σ(Y) = B(ℝ²)

Thus:
σ(X) ⊂ σ(Y)

---

## Marginal Densities

X ~ N(0,1):

f_X(x) = (1/√(2π)) exp(-x²/2)

Y joint density:

f_Y(t,p) = (1/4π) exp(-t²/2 - p²/8)

---

## Probabilities

P(X ≤ 0) = 0.5  
P(X > 1) ≈ 0.1587  

P(Y ∈ (-∞,0] × ℝ) = 0.5  

P(Y ∈ [-1,1] × [-2,2])  
= (2Φ(1) − 1)² ≈ 0.466  

---

## Conditional Distributions

X | Y=(t,p) → deterministic:

X = t

Y | X=t:

Y = (t, P), P ~ N(0,4)

---

## Entropy

h(X) = ½ ln(2πe) ≈ 1.4189  

h(Y) = ln(2πe) + ln(2) ≈ 3.5310  

h(Y|X) = h(P) = ½ ln(8πe) ≈ 2.1121  

h(X|Y) → −∞ (degenerate distribution)

---

## Interpretation

- X gives only temperature info  
- Y gives full system state  
- Knowing Y removes all uncertainty about X  
- Continuous entropy behaves differently from discrete case

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import norm

# Parameters
mu_T, sigma_T = 0, 1
mu_P, sigma_P = 0, 2   # variance = 4

# Grid
t = np.linspace(-4, 4, 400)
p = np.linspace(-8, 8, 400)
T, P = np.meshgrid(t, p)

# Joint density
joint = norm.pdf(T, mu_T, sigma_T) * norm.pdf(P, mu_P, sigma_P)

# Marginal of X
fx = norm.pdf(t, mu_T, sigma_T)

# Conditional P|X
cond_p = norm.pdf(p, mu_P, sigma_P)

# Plot
fig = plt.figure(figsize=(12, 10))

# Joint density
ax1 = fig.add_subplot(221)
cp = ax1.contourf(T, P, joint, levels=20)
ax1.set_title("Joint Density of Y = (T, P)")
ax1.set_xlabel("t")
ax1.set_ylabel("p")

# Marginal X
ax2 = fig.add_subplot(222)
ax2.plot(t, fx)
ax2.set_title("Marginal Density of X")

# Conditional P|X
ax3 = fig.add_subplot(223)
ax3.plot(p, cond_p)
ax3.set_title("P | X = t")

# Sigma-algebra visualization
ax4 = fig.add_subplot(224)

# vertical strip (σ(X))
ax4.fill_betweenx(p, -1, 1, alpha=0.3)

# rectangle (σ(Y))
ax4.add_patch(plt.Rectangle((-1.5, -3), 3, 6, fill=False))

ax4.set_title("σ(X) vs σ(Y)")
ax4.set_xlim(-4, 4)
ax4.set_ylim(-8, 8)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
