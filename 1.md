# Q1) The Probability of Shooting at a Target

## Problem Statement

A point is chosen at random from a disk of radius 10. Let **A** be the event that the point lies within 1 unit of the boundary.

1. Model the experiment assuming the point is **uniformly distributed with respect to area**, and compute P(A).
2. Model the experiment assuming the **distance to the point from the center is chosen uniformly** from [0, 10], and the direction is chosen independently and uniformly from [0, 2π), and compute P(A).
3. Explain why the two answers differ.

---

## Setup

Let the disk be:

```math
\Omega = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x^2 + y^2 \leq 100\}
```

Let **A** be the event that the chosen point lies within 1 unit of the boundary. Since the boundary is the circle of radius 10, the point must have distance **at least 9** and **at most 10** from the center. Thus:

```math
A = \{(x, y) \in \Omega : 9 \leq \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \leq 10\}
```

So **A** is the **outer annulus** of the disk.

---

## Part 1 — Uniform Distribution with Respect to Area

In this model, equal areas are assigned equal probabilities. The probability of any event is its area divided by the total area of the disk.

**Total area of the disk:**

```math
\text{Area}(\Omega) = \pi (10)^2 = 100\pi
```

**Area of event A** (annulus between radii 9 and 10):

```math
\text{Area}(A) = \pi(10)^2 - \pi(9)^2 = 100\pi - 81\pi = 19\pi
```

**Probability:**

```math
P(A) = \frac{\text{Area}(A)}{\text{Area}(\Omega)} = \frac{19\pi}{100\pi} = \frac{19}{100}
```

> **Answer: P(A) = 19/100**

---

## Part 2 — Uniform Distribution of the Radius

In this model, there are two independent variables:

- **Radius** r: chosen uniformly from [0, 10]
- **Angle** θ: chosen uniformly from [0, 2π)

Since the angle is independent of the radius, the direction does not affect whether the point falls near the boundary. We focus entirely on r.

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---|
| Total length of radius interval [0, 10] | 10 |
| Favorable interval for A: r in [9, 10] | 1 |

**Probability:**

```math
P(A) = \frac{\text{Favorable Length}}{\text{Total Length}} = \frac{10 - 9}{10 - 0} = \frac{1}{10}
```

> **Answer: P(A) = 1/10**

---

## Part 3 — Why the Two Answers Differ

The two answers differ because **the phrase "a point is chosen at random from a disk" does not determine a unique probability model**. The event A is the same geometric set in both cases, but the two models assign probability differently.

### Model 1 — Area-Uniform

Probability is proportional to **area**. The outer annulus [9, 10] is geometrically large, so it receives a high probability:

```math
P(A) = \frac{19}{100} = 19\%
```

Points near the boundary are *more likely* because they occupy more area.

### Model 2 — Radius-Uniform

Probability is proportional to **radial length** only. The interval [9, 10] is just 1 out of 10 equal units:

```math
P(A) = \frac{1}{10} = 10\%
```

This model ignores the fact that outer radii sweep more area.

### Root Cause

The key difference is the **area element** in polar coordinates:

```math
dA = r \, dr \, d\theta
```

The factor of **r** means that larger radii contribute *more area per unit of radius*. The area-uniform model accounts for this; the radius-uniform model ignores it.

> **In short:** the two models are different probability measures on the same sample space. The same event can have different probabilities under different measures. Probabilities are not determined by geometry alone — they also depend on *how* "random" is defined.

---

## Physical Interpretation

| Scenario | Appropriate Model | P(A) |
|---|---|---|
| Random dart throwing (no directional/radial bias) | Area-uniform | 19/100 |
| Two-step procedure: pick r uniformly, then pick θ | Radius-uniform | 1/10 |

In most physical experiments, **"randomly chosen point in a disk"** refers to the **area-uniform** model — because randomly scattered points naturally land in proportion to area. The radius-uniform model represents an artificial two-step sampling procedure, not ordinary geometric randomness.
